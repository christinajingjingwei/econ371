Can We Afford Decarbonization? Navigating the Costs and Challenges

Topic Climate Change Region Environmental Protection

Trump's re-election as president of the United States suddenly brought decarbonization to people’s attention again. He publicly criticized the Paris Agreement during his first presidency, believing the agreement harmed the interests of the United States and cost a huge amount of money. Trump also took the lead in withdrawing from the agreement during his first term (Holden, 2019). After Biden was elected, the United States rejoined. Now, all eyes are on whether Trump, who is reelected, will again spearhead the exit from the agreement.

In 2015, at the United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP21) held in Paris, the Paris Agreement was reached (UNFCC, 2015). Member states agreed to carry out international cooperation to curb global temperature rise and the agreement serves as a collective effort to mitigate the impacts of climate change.

Is the Paris Agreement unachievable?

Under the Paris Agreement, developed countries are supposed to provide at least $100 billion annually to help developing countries reduce their emissions and adapt to climate change. This was one of many items that Trump said puts the US at a disadvantage. Moreover, the consensus reached by the United Nations in a recent meeting is that decarbonization comes at an exorbitant price. It is estimated that for developing countries, despite the efforts, there is still an annual finance gap of $359 required to slow down the rate of global warming (Mishra, 2024).

However, regarding the 2°C target under the Paris agreement, the major expense of decarbonization is concentrated in the beginning. The 2°C target from the Paris Agreement is particularly demanding in terms of the need for a more concentrated early investment in clean energy. Energy investment in clean energy needs to go up from $1 trillion in 2020, reach $4 trillion by 2040, and then start to grow more slowly. Meanwhile, fossil fuel investments would decrease and operating costs in relation to falling demand would start to drop.

Timing is, therefore, the critical question of investment. At a later stage, the burden is not expected to be that heavy. With larger investments on clean energy and sustainable improvements, it might be reduced below 1.8°C. The benefits from fewer losses from climatic disasters could outweigh the expenditures, even if the temperature drops by just a fraction of a degree. For instance, China's EV sector is significantly reducing its carbon footprint as part of the clean electricity strategy. China is actively promoting the use of green electricity and intends to reduce industry decarbonization by 95% by 2060. (Sandström and Zhu, 2024).

Challenges ahead for decarbonization

However, challenges remain to achieve the target. For example, in respect of reducing agricultural emissions, relevant technologies may not be mature yet. Thus, reduction costs for future emissions are difficult to estimate. Insufficient policies and implementation can also be an issue. For example, carbon taxes can be used to reduce carbon reduction costs, but due to political pressure, more expensive subsidies are usually adopted. The government can also be favorable to industries of fossil fuel or can protect local companies and enhance extra expenditures.

Although the economic burden of decarbonization seems daunting, with the right policies and continued technological advancements, the cost of decarbonization can be reduced with collaboration. The potential benefits of decarbonization may far outweigh the initial investment.
